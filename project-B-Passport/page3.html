The map of Poland has undergone significant changes throughout history, shaped by wars, partitions, and shifting geopolitical alliances. At its zenith during the 16th and 17th centuries, the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth was a vast and powerful state that stretched from the Baltic Sea to the Black Sea. However, the partitions of Poland in the late 18th century by neighboring powers—Russia, Prussia, and Austria—resulted in the country disappearing from the map for over a century. Poland regained independence after World War I, but its borders continued to fluctuate due to conflicts and treaties with neighboring countries. The most notable changes occurred during and after World War II, when Poland's eastern borders shifted westward, and its territories were redrawn under Soviet influence.
